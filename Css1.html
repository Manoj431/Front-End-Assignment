Grass

Grasses are an important food for many animals, like deer, buffalo, cattle, mice, grasshoppers, caterpillars, and many other grazers.
Unlike other plants, grasses grow from the bottom, so when animals eat grass they usually do not destroy the part that grows.
Without grass, dirt can wash away into rivers (erosion)


Grass is a plant with narrow leaves growing from the base. A common kind of grass is used to cover the ground in a lawn and other places. Grass gets water from the roots in the ground. Grasses are monocotyledon plant, herbaceous plants.

Evolution of grass

Graminoids include some of the most versatile plant life-forms.
They became widespread toward the end of the Cretaceous. Fossilized dinosaur dung (coprolites) have been found containing grass phytoliths (silica stones inside grass leaves).
Grasses have adapted to conditions in lush rain forests, dry deserts, cold mountains and even intertidal habitats, and are now the most widespread plant type.
Grass is a valuable source of food and energy for many animals.
